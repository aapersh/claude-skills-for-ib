# Market and Sector Brief

## Purpose

Build a fact-based, banker-grade sector view that supports a transaction story — sizing, growth, structure, cycle position, deal activity, and capital markets backdrop — without padding it with generic industry color.

The brief is the page a board reads to know whether the moment is right and whether the sector logic supports the price. It is not a strategy-consulting industry report; it is a banker's working document anchored in deal-relevant facts.

## Governing Principle

**The sector page exists to support the transaction, not to demonstrate sector knowledge.**

Every fact on the brief must connect to a deal decision: pricing, timing, buyer logic, financing, or strategic rationale. If a fact doesn't connect, cut it.

## The Five Sections Every Sector Brief Must Contain

A sector brief is incomplete without all five. Each answers a question that a client, IC, or counterparty will ask within the first 15 minutes.

1. **Size and growth** — how big is the market, how fast is it growing, on what trajectory
2. **Structure** — who are the players, what is the concentration, where are the profit pools
3. **Cycle** — where is the sector in its cycle and what does that mean for pricing
4. **M&A activity** — what has traded, at what multiples, with what buyer types, and what does it say about appetite
5. **Capital markets backdrop** — what is the financing reality for transactions in this sector right now

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Define the sector and sub-sector taxonomy precisely

Most sector pages fail at the definition. A "healthcare services" page is useless because the multiples in dialysis, behavioral health, ambulatory surgery, and post-acute care have nothing to do with each other.

For the relevant deal, define:

- **Primary sector** (broad)
- **Sub-sector** (the level at which competitive dynamics actually operate)
- **Micro-vertical** (where the target sits and where the directly relevant comps sit)

Write the sub-sector definition as one sentence: *"Companies that [do this activity] for [these customers] under [this revenue model] in [this geographic scope]."*

Anything outside that sentence is context, not comp. Treat it accordingly.

### Step 2 — Size the market with two methods and reconcile

Pull market size from at least two independent sources (industry analyst reports, regulatory data, trade associations, equity research). They will disagree.

Then build a bottom-up sanity check:

```
Market size ≈ Number of buyers × Average spend × Frequency
```

If the top-down and bottom-up differ by more than ~30%, investigate the gap before quoting either number. The investigation is more valuable than the resolution — it usually reveals a definitional mismatch that affects the deal story.

For growth:

- Historical 3–5 year CAGR
- Forward 3–5 year consensus / analyst estimates
- Drivers of growth, decomposed: volume growth, price growth, mix shift, regulatory tailwind, secular shift
- An honest read on whether the consensus growth rate has supply behind it (capacity, capital, labor) or is wishful

Decomposed growth is what makes the page credible. "Market growing 8%" is a slide; "Market growing 8% — 3% volume, 4% price, 1% mix" is a thesis.

### Step 3 — Map the structure and concentration

For the relevant sub-sector:

- **Top 5 / Top 10 share** — concentration ratio (CR5, CR10) with sources
- **HHI** if data permits, especially in regulated sectors
- **Tier structure:** the scale tiers in the market — bulge, mid-market, regional, local
- **Profit pool distribution:** where does the EBITDA actually sit? (Often not where the revenue sits — distributors, platforms, and gatekeepers capture disproportionate margin)

For each major player, a one-line description: scale, geographic footprint, recent strategic moves, ownership (public / sponsor / family / strategic). This becomes input to the buyer universe later.

### Step 4 — Read the cycle position

Sectors are cyclical at different frequencies. The brief must explicitly answer: where are we in the cycle?

Cycle inputs to assess:

- **Demand cycle:** is end demand at trend, above trend, or below trend? What inning of the cycle?
- **Supply cycle:** is capacity tight or loose? Are new entrants being financed?
- **Pricing power:** are players taking price or absorbing inflation?
- **Inventory and channel state:** is there channel stuffing, destocking, or normal sell-through?
- **Margin position:** are sector margins at peak, mid-cycle, or trough?

Frame the cycle position explicitly: "Sector EBITDA margins are ~250bps above 10-year average; we treat this as mid-to-late cycle." Then carry that view into the multiple discussion — late-cycle businesses should not be valued on peak earnings × peak multiples.

### Step 5 — Pull and read M&A activity

Activity tells you what the market is willing to do. For the last 24–36 months in the sub-sector:

- **Deal count by quarter** — accelerating, decelerating, steady
- **Median multiple paid** — EV/EBITDA or EV/revenue, with the 25th–75th percentile range
- **Buyer mix** — % strategic vs. % financial; this often shifts dramatically with the credit cycle
- **Deal size distribution** — where is the activity (micro / mid / large)
- **Cross-border share** — is the buyer universe global or domestic
- **Walked / busted deals** — what got announced and didn't close, and why

Three diagnostic patterns:

- **Rising count, rising multiples, strategic-led:** sector in a consolidation wave
- **Rising count, compressing multiples, sponsor-led:** financial buyer activity supporting volume while strategics step back
- **Falling count, wide multiple spread:** late cycle with bid-ask gap; sellers waiting, buyers cautious

The pattern shapes process design and pricing expectations.

### Step 6 — Read the capital markets backdrop

A sector view is incomplete without the financing reality for this exact sub-sector right now:

- **Public equity market:** sector index performance, IPO activity, follow-on activity, recent issuances
- **Sponsor activity:** dry powder targeting the sector, recent fund closes, sponsor add-on activity
- **Leveraged finance:** what is achievable? Senior leverage levels, total leverage levels, all-in coupons, covenant package realities
- **Strategic balance sheets:** net leverage trend for major strategic buyers, recent capital allocation announcements
- **Rates and credit spreads:** investment grade and high yield spreads, base rate environment

The financing reality is what determines what a sponsor can pay and what a strategic can absorb. A pitch deck that quotes 6.0x leverage when the market is offering 5.0x will be exposed within minutes by any sophisticated counterparty.

### Step 7 — Surface the "why now" and the "why not now"

A sector brief that ends in description, not implication, is incomplete. The closing slide must state:

- **Why now is the right window for a transaction:** the 2–3 specific dynamics making this moment good
- **Why now is the wrong window:** the 1–2 risks that argue for waiting
- **The break-points:** what would change in the next 6–12 months that would flip the window?

This is the page the client uses to decide whether to move. Without an explicit view, the brief is research, not advice.

### Step 8 — Compress the brief to deal-relevant outputs

A working sector file is rich. The deliverable is compressed. For most mandates, produce:

- **One executive summary slide:** size, growth, structure, cycle, activity, capital markets — six bullets
- **One sizing slide:** TAM with growth decomposition
- **One structure slide:** concentration map, top players, profit pools
- **One M&A activity slide:** deal count trend, multiple distribution, buyer mix
- **One capital markets slide:** financing reality, sponsor activity, strategic balance sheet capacity
- **One "why now" slide:** the window argument

That is six pages, plus appendix. More pages means the brief is hiding behind volume.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test

1. **"The growth rate is a survivorship illusion."** Is the headline growth driven by a few winners while the median company in the sector is flat or declining?
2. **"The multiple environment is being held up by sponsor activity that depends on financing conditions."** What happens to multiples if HY spreads widen 100bps?
3. **"Strategics are inactive because they are between cycles, not because they are uninterested."** Is the strategic balance sheet capacity actually there for a return-to-activity?
4. **"The sub-sector definition is too broad."** Are we mixing companies with different unit economics into one market story?
5. **"The 'why now' rests on a single catalyst that may not arrive."** What in the brief depends on regulatory action, a specific election outcome, or a competitor decision we don't control?

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] Sub-sector defined in one sentence, with the relevant micro-vertical called out
- [ ] Market size from at least two sources, with a bottom-up sanity check, and the gap explained
- [ ] Growth rate decomposed into volume, price, mix, and tailwinds
- [ ] Top-5 / top-10 concentration ratios with sources
- [ ] Profit pool mapping shows where margin sits, not just where revenue sits
- [ ] Cycle position called explicitly, with margin-vs-historical-average referenced
- [ ] M&A activity shows deal count trend, multiple distribution, buyer mix, and walked deals
- [ ] Capital markets backdrop covers public equity, sponsor activity, leveraged finance, and strategic balance sheet capacity
- [ ] "Why now" and "why not now" are both stated, with break-points named
- [ ] Every fact on the page connects to a deal decision; nothing is on the page only for color

## Common Failure Modes

- **Generic industry color.** A sector page that could be lifted into any pitch for any company in the sector. Useless. The page must tilt toward the transaction.
- **TAM theater.** Quoting a $50B TAM where the addressable opportunity is $500M. Size to the relevant slice.
- **Stale activity data.** Showing deal activity through Q-minus-2 when the credit cycle has shifted twice since. Activity data ages faster than fundamental data.
- **Buyer-blind activity reading.** Reporting a median multiple without flagging that 80% of recent deals were sponsor-led at the time strategics were sidelined.
- **No cycle view.** Treating current margins and current multiples as the normal state of the world. Every cycle ends.
- **Ignoring the financing reality.** Building a buyer angle that assumes leverage availability that the credit market does not currently offer.
- **"Why now" without a counter.** Confirmation pages that don't acknowledge what could go wrong. They lose credibility the moment a sharp committee member pushes back.

## Deliverable

A six-page sector brief covering sub-sector definition, sized market with decomposed growth, structure and profit pool map, cycle position, M&A activity trend with multiple distribution and buyer mix, capital markets backdrop, and a "why now" page with explicit break-points — supported by a working file with sourced data, screening logs, and the bottom-up sizing build.
