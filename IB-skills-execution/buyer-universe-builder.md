# Buyer Universe Builder

## Purpose

Build a buyer list that has a deal thesis attached to every name — segmented by acquisition rationale, sized by capacity to pay, and tiered by process posture — so the seller can choose between a broad auction, a limited process, or a targeted negotiation with eyes open.

The output is not a long list of logos. It is a working buyer file where every named entity has a documented rationale, a capacity view, and a process intensity recommendation.

## Governing Principle

**Every name on the list answers two questions: why would they buy this, and can they actually pay for it? Names that fail either test are noise.**

A broad buyer list with no thesis behind each name is the most common failure in sell-side processes. It generates outreach volume, NDA volume, and IOI volume — and then those IOIs collapse in round two because the underlying logic was never there.

## The Three Cuts Every Buyer Universe Must Pass Through

A working buyer list is constructed in three passes. Each pass tightens the set.

1. **Universe cut** — every conceivable buyer, segmented by archetype
2. **Logic cut** — only those with a credible acquisition rationale, written down
3. **Capacity cut** — only those who can pay the asking range given balance sheet, fund size, and credit reality

The final list is the intersection of all three. Tier the result by process posture.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Build the strategic universe by adjacency

Strategics fall into archetypes by relationship to the target's core business:

- **Direct competitors:** same customer, same product — consolidation rationale
- **Adjacent product strategics:** same customer, complementary product — cross-sell / bundle rationale
- **Adjacent customer strategics:** different customer using related capability — scale / capability transfer rationale
- **Vertical strategics:** upstream or downstream in the value chain — integration rationale
- **Geographic expanders:** strong in adjacent geographies — footprint extension rationale
- **Platform consolidators:** strategics actively rolling up the sub-sector — known acquisition program

For each archetype, name the specific companies. Use the sector brief, the target's competitor list, recent M&A activity in the sub-sector, and earnings-call M&A commentary to populate.

Aim for 30–80 names at this stage, with archetype tagging.

### Step 2 — Build the sponsor universe by portfolio and thesis fit

Sponsors get cut by three filters:

- **Portfolio fit:** sponsors with existing platforms in this sub-sector for whom this target is an add-on, or sponsors with a stated sub-sector thesis
- **Fund stage:** funds in active deployment mode (typically year 2–4 of a fund) rather than late-deployment or harvest mode
- **Check size fit:** equity check size matches the target — too-large funds will not engage with sub-scale deals, too-small funds will not stretch above their typical equity bite

Source from:

- Fund websites and stated investment criteria
- Recent fund closes and known dry powder
- Sponsor portfolio websites (existing platforms in adjacent verticals)
- Recent sponsor activity in the sub-sector (PitchBook, Mergermarket sponsor deal flow)
- Prior approaches by sponsors to the target or to known competitors

For each named sponsor, identify the deal partner most likely to lead — sponsor coverage usually has a single relevant point of contact per firm.

Sponsor list typically 20–60 names.

### Step 3 — Write the one-line acquisition rationale for every name

This is the discipline that separates a real buyer universe from a logo list. For every named strategic and sponsor, write one sentence:

*"[Buyer name] should buy [target] because [specific rationale tied to their stated strategy, recent activity, or known capability gap]."*

If the rationale cannot be written in one sentence with a specific reason, the name should not be on the list. Generic rationales fail:

- ❌ "Strategic fit"
- ❌ "Scale benefits"
- ❌ "Complementary"
- ✅ "Already announced intent to scale European footprint; target gives them ~$200M revenue in regions where they have no presence today, with overlapping enterprise customer base"
- ✅ "Sponsor portfolio company [X] is the obvious bolt-on platform; CEO publicly stated targeting $1B revenue, currently at $620M, target adds $280M with margin parity"

The rationale paragraph for the top 10–15 names should run 2–4 sentences each, with capacity sizing.

### Step 4 — Size the capacity to pay

For each name surviving the logic cut, build a capacity view:

**For strategics:**

- Current net leverage / EBITDA
- Stated leverage targets and recent credit-rating posture
- Cash on balance sheet
- Recent capital allocation pattern (M&A, buybacks, dividends — what is the marginal capital used for?)
- Implied incremental debt capacity at target leverage levels
- Stock currency availability (P/E, recent share repurchase, dilution tolerance)
- Implied accretion/dilution at the contemplated price (preview only; the full test is in the accretion-dilution skill)

If a strategic would need to take leverage materially above its stated target to fund the deal, the deal is hard for them unless they can defend it as transformative. Flag accordingly.

**For sponsors:**

- Fund vintage and remaining dry powder
- Typical equity check size from recent deal history
- Maximum check size from fund size (typically 10–20% of fund for a platform deal)
- Co-invest capacity from LPs (relevant for larger deals)
- Existing portfolio leverage / capacity for an add-on through an existing platform

A sponsor with $4B in a Fund VII typically writes $400–600M equity checks. A $1.5B equity deal is outside their range unless co-investment is available.

### Step 5 — Tier the list by process posture

Now segment the surviving list into process tiers:

- **Tier 1 — Lead buyers (5–10 names):** Strongest rationale, capacity confirmed, prior engagement either direct or via competitor processes. These get personal outreach from the senior banker. Often the eventual winner sits here.
- **Tier 2 — Strong logic, less certain (10–20 names):** Real rationale but capacity is a stretch or process posture is uncertain. Standard outreach with bespoke teaser positioning.
- **Tier 3 — Broaden the field (15–30 names):** Plausible logic, fills in the auction for competitive tension. Standard process outreach, no bespoke handling.
- **Tier 4 — Long shots:** Named but flagged as low conversion likelihood. Sometimes excluded entirely if the process is limited.

Tier allocation is a strategic choice that depends on the process type:

- **Broad auction:** all four tiers; 60+ names contacted
- **Limited auction:** Tiers 1–2; 15–25 names contacted
- **Targeted process:** Tier 1 only; 5–10 names contacted
- **Negotiated:** single Tier 1 buyer; sometimes a stalking horse

### Step 6 — Handle conflicts, fit issues, and process risk

Surface the issues before outreach starts. For each name in Tiers 1 and 2:

- **Antitrust risk:** is this combination a horizontal merger that triggers Phase 2 review? What is the deal certainty cost?
- **Regulatory / national security risk:** CFIUS, FDI screening, sector-specific regulatory approvals
- **Customer overlap risk:** is this buyer also a major customer of the target, with switching risk if the deal doesn't happen?
- **Information sensitivity:** is this buyer a direct competitor whose access to the data room creates real strategic risk if they don't bid?
- **Process behavior:** has this buyer historically gone late-stage and then re-traded? Re-traded at signing? Failed to close?

For names with material issues, document the mitigation plan: clean team protocols, late-stage information release, signed non-circumvention, sponsor co-investor structuring.

### Step 7 — Sequence the outreach

Outreach order matters. Two rules:

- **High-confidence Tier 1 names go first.** They set the pace and the price expectation. If a strong Tier 1 declines early, recalibrate the asking range before going broader.
- **Direct competitor strategics go last or via clean-team.** Their information value to themselves is highest; their process commitment is often lowest.

Build the outreach Gantt: which name gets the call when, who makes the call (senior banker for Tier 1, deal team for lower tiers), what the bespoke positioning is for each.

### Step 8 — Maintain the buyer file as a living document

A buyer universe is not a one-time deliverable; it is the working spine of the process. Throughout the process, maintain:

- **Engagement status:** outreach made, NDA signed, CIM sent, in-process, IOI received, advanced, declined
- **Decline reasons:** specifically captured (price, fit, capacity, timing, internal) — these inform the next round and the next mandate
- **Movement between tiers:** buyers who upgrade or downgrade based on engagement quality
- **Decision-maker mapping:** the specific individual at each buyer driving the process on their side

The post-process buyer file is also one of the most valuable artifacts the bank retains — it informs every future mandate in the sub-sector.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test

1. **"The list is too broad and not anchored in acquisition rationale."** Strip every name without a one-sentence specific rationale. What survives? If the list collapses to a third of its size, the original was a logo list, not a buyer universe.
2. **"The Tier 1 list is being padded with names that won't engage."** Which Tier 1 buyers have a real track record of completing deals in this sub-sector recently? Past behavior beats stated strategy.
3. **"We are missing the cross-border angle."** What international strategics have a credible reason to enter, and what is their balance sheet capacity?
4. **"The sponsor list is right by sector but wrong by fund stage."** Which named sponsors are actually in deployment mode for this size deal in this vintage?
5. **"The most aggressive bidder will come from outside the obvious list."** Adjacent-industry strategics, sponsor consortiums, and emerging market acquirers often pay the most because the deal is rarer for them. Have we surfaced them?

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] Every name on the list has a one-sentence specific acquisition rationale documented
- [ ] Strategic universe segmented by archetype (direct / adjacent product / adjacent customer / vertical / geographic / consolidator)
- [ ] Sponsor universe filtered by portfolio fit, fund stage, and check size
- [ ] Capacity-to-pay built for every name in Tiers 1 and 2 (leverage, dry powder, stock currency)
- [ ] Tiering is explicit: Tier 1 / 2 / 3 / 4 with documented criteria, not just a sorted list
- [ ] Antitrust, regulatory, customer overlap, and information sensitivity flagged for each Tier 1–2 name
- [ ] Outreach sequence documented with who calls whom and when
- [ ] Direct competitor strategics are either deferred or handled via clean team
- [ ] Cross-border and adjacent-industry buyers explicitly considered, not just defaulted out
- [ ] List sized to the process type (broad / limited / targeted / negotiated), not maximized

## Common Failure Modes

- **Logo grid.** A buyer page with 60 logos and no rationale. Looks like work; isn't.
- **Sector-only filter.** Restricting the list to companies in the same NAICS code. The best buyers often sit in adjacent industries.
- **No capacity test.** Naming strategics whose balance sheet cannot accommodate the deal at any reasonable price. Wastes everyone's time when they decline at IOI.
- **Sponsor list by name recognition.** Including the largest funds because they are visible, regardless of check-size fit or sub-sector relevance.
- **Tier inflation.** Putting too many names in Tier 1 to make the process look richer than it is. Senior bankers can tell the difference within minutes.
- **Ignoring conflicts.** Outreach to a direct competitor without a clean-team structure, then surprise when proprietary information surfaces in competitive moves 18 months later.
- **Static buyer file.** Treating the initial list as fixed. Real buyer universes shift as decline reasons come back. The list should evolve through the process.
- **Headlines from the sponsor's website.** Taking the sponsor's stated sub-sector focus at face value without checking what they have actually closed recently. Stated strategy ≠ revealed strategy.

## Deliverable

A tiered buyer universe with strategic and sponsor names, each with a one-sentence acquisition rationale, a capacity-to-pay view for Tier 1–2 names, antitrust and process-risk flags, an outreach sequence with named bankers and bespoke positioning per Tier 1 name, and a process-type recommendation (broad / limited / targeted / negotiated) tied to the universe shape.
