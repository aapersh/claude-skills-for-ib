# Deal Process Manager

## Purpose

Run a transaction from kickoff to close with the discipline that keeps the deal on the critical path, keeps the workstreams synchronized, and keeps the committee process honest — so the deal doesn't lose to the avoidable failure modes (missed milestones, surprise diligence, IC re-trade, sign-to-close drift).

The output is a living process plan: a sequenced workstream schedule, a committee cadence, a risk register that gets updated weekly, and a critical-path view that tells the team where the next 14 days actually matter.

## Governing Principle

**Most deals don't fail at the negotiation. They fail at the workstream that wasn't on the critical path until it was.**

A senior associate's job is to make the invisible critical path visible, every week, so the team works on the things that determine whether the deal closes — not on the things that produce the most documents.

## The Four Pillars Every Process Must Have

A process plan that lacks any of these is structurally weak. Each pillar maps to a specific deal-failure mode if it is missing.

1. **Workstream architecture** — every workstream named, sequenced, owned, dependency-mapped
2. **Critical-path management** — explicit identification of which workstream is binding the timeline
3. **Committee and approval cadence** — IC, board, regulatory approvals on a calendar, not a hope
4. **Risk register** — live document of issues, with severity, owner, mitigation, and target close date

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Design the process to the situation

Process type is a strategic choice that shapes everything downstream:

| Process type | When to use | Typical timeline (signing) |
|---|---|---|
| Broad auction | Strong asset, broad strategic + sponsor interest, seller can absorb leak risk | 16–24 weeks |
| Limited auction | High-quality asset, narrower buyer logic, manageable confidentiality | 14–20 weeks |
| Targeted process | Sub-scale buyer universe, specific strategic logic, sensitive to leak | 10–16 weeks |
| Negotiated / single-buyer | Identified strategic partner, pre-existing relationship, structural deal | 8–14 weeks |
| Dual-track (IPO + sale) | High-quality asset with both IPO and strategic exit credibility | 24–36 weeks (parallel) |

Choose the process type explicitly. Document the trade-offs: a broad auction maximizes price discovery but elongates timeline and increases leak risk; a targeted process compresses time but caps competitive tension.

### Step 2 — Map the workstreams and ownership

Every deal has a recognizable workstream architecture. Name them, assign owners, and tag dependencies:

- **Commercial / strategic:** valuation, buyer outreach, IOI/LOI receipt, negotiation, deal terms
- **Financial diligence:** QofE, financial model, data room financial tab, management presentation financials
- **Legal:** definitive agreements, disclosure schedules, regs, transaction structure
- **Tax:** transaction structuring, tax due diligence, jurisdictional planning
- **Commercial diligence:** customer references, market diligence, technical diligence
- **HR / management:** retention, compensation, change-of-control, management presentations
- **Regulatory:** antitrust filings (HSR, EC, others), CFIUS / FDI, sector-specific approvals
- **Financing:** debt commitment, equity issuance, ratings, syndication
- **IT / cyber / data:** technology diligence, data privacy diligence, cyber assessment
- **Communications:** press release, employee communications, customer messaging, analyst dialogue
- **Integration / Day 1 planning:** for strategic acquirers — clean teams, IMO setup, Day 1 readiness
- **Closing mechanics:** funds flow, deliverables, regulatory clearances, conditions precedent

For each workstream, name the lead (specific person, not function), the deliverable, and the dependencies on other workstreams.

### Step 3 — Build the timeline as a Gantt with critical path

Lay out the workstreams against a week-by-week timeline. For each workstream, identify:

- **Start trigger:** what must happen before this workstream begins
- **Duration:** realistic working days, including review cycles
- **End deliverable:** the artifact that signifies completion
- **Downstream dependencies:** what other workstreams need this output

The critical path is the longest chain of dependent workstreams. Typically, on a public-target deal, the critical path runs through: definitive agreement negotiation → regulatory filings → regulatory clearance → closing. On a private deal, it usually runs through: management presentation → confirmatory diligence → definitive agreement → closing conditions.

Identify the critical-path workstream explicitly, each week. The team's attention should sit on the critical path. Workstreams off the critical path are not unimportant; they are just not the binding constraint this week.

### Step 4 — Lay in the committee and approval cadence

Approvals are the most common source of avoidable delay. Map them upfront:

- **Seller-side committees:** board approval (with notice period requirements), shareholder approval if required, audit committee for fairness, special committee if conflicted
- **Buyer-side committees:** IC schedule, board approval, financing committee if leveraged, integration committee for strategics
- **Regulatory approvals:** HSR (30 days unless Second Request), EC merger control (25 working days Phase I, 90+ Phase II), CFIUS (45-day initial review + 45-day investigation), sector-specific (insurance, banking, defense, telecom, utilities)
- **Third-party consents:** material customer change-of-control, lender consents, landlord consents, joint-venture partner consents

For each, document: timing requirement, notice requirement, materials needed, owner. Build these into the Gantt as dependencies, not afterthoughts.

The IC cadence on the buyer side is particularly important. A typical strategic IC runs every 2–4 weeks; sponsor IC runs weekly during active processes. Know the cadence and back-solve from it.

### Step 5 — Maintain the risk register as a living document

The risk register is the single most underused process management tool. Build it at kickoff and update weekly:

| Issue | Severity (H/M/L) | Workstream | Owner | Mitigation | Status | Target close |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Customer concentration in top-3, contract renewal in 12 months | H | Commercial diligence | [Name] | Schedule customer references, get renewal status | Open | Week 6 |
| Antitrust risk in EU on horizontal overlap | H | Regulatory | [Name] | Engage antitrust counsel, prep efficiencies analysis | Open | Week 4 |
| Sponsor financing covenant on portfolio company | M | Financing | [Name] | Lender amendment letter prepared | Open | Week 8 |

Severity should be calibrated to deal impact, not topic difficulty. An issue is "H" if unresolved it could kill the deal or trigger a material price reset. "M" if it would condition the LOI / SPA. "L" if it is a normal-course diligence item.

Items don't fall off the register; they get closed with a date and a note on the resolution. The register is the audit trail of how the deal got managed.

### Step 6 — Run the weekly cadence

A live deal runs on a weekly rhythm:

- **Monday — internal deal team:** review the risk register, walk the critical path, assign workstream actions for the week
- **Mid-week — workstream check-ins:** legal, financial diligence, regulatory, financing — each separately
- **End of week — client / counterparty update:** what got done, what is binding, what we need from them next week
- **Weekly client memo:** one page, written. Status by workstream, critical path, risk register changes, decisions needed. The discipline of writing it forces clarity.

The weekly memo is also the audit trail and the alignment mechanism with the senior banker and the client. Skip a week of memos and the deal drifts; resume them and the drift becomes visible.

### Step 7 — Manage the sign-to-close period as its own process

Deals don't end at signing. The sign-to-close period (typically 60–180 days for public deals, 30–90 days for private) has its own process discipline:

- **Closing conditions tracker:** every CP, with owner, status, target close date
- **Regulatory clearance management:** HSR waiting period (or Second Request response), EC clearance, CFIUS clearance
- **Third-party consent tracker:** which consents are outstanding, what is the path to each
- **Funds flow and closing mechanics:** lender funding, escrow, working capital adjustment mechanics, paying-agent setup
- **Day 1 communications:** press release, employee announcement, customer outreach, analyst messaging
- **Disruption monitoring:** any change in business, customer churn, key talent departure, market shift that could trigger MAC discussion

The sign-to-close failure mode is drift: the deal team disbands prematurely, the workstreams lose ownership, and the closing list isn't actively managed. Resist this.

### Step 8 — Run the post-mortem honestly

At close, run a post-mortem with the full deal team:

- What did we predict well? Where did the original process plan hold?
- Where did the critical path shift, and did we recognize it in time?
- Which risks became real, and how did we handle them? Which risks did we miss?
- Which workstreams were under-resourced? Which were over-resourced?
- What would we do differently for the next mandate in this sub-sector / size / structure?

The post-mortem produces the process IP that makes the next mandate easier. Skipping the post-mortem is how a bank runs the same deal three times without getting better at it.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test

1. **"The critical path is not the workstream the team thinks it is."** Walk the Gantt. Where is the longest dependency chain? It is often regulatory or financing, not commercial negotiation.
2. **"The committee cadence is set up for the buyer's IC schedule, not ours."** Are we structuring milestones around the seller's board calendar, the buyer's IC calendar, or our own?
3. **"The risk register is current as of week 1."** Is it being updated weekly with new issues that emerge in diligence, or has it become a static document?
4. **"The regulatory clearance timeline is wishful."** Are we assuming standard HSR clearance when the horizontal overlap suggests a Second Request? Are we assuming EC Phase I when the market shares suggest Phase II?
5. **"Sign-to-close has no owner."** Is there a named lead for the post-signing period, with weekly cadence maintained, or does the team disband and the closing list drift?

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] Process type explicitly chosen (broad / limited / targeted / negotiated / dual-track) with trade-off documented
- [ ] Workstream architecture mapped with named owners (people, not functions) for every stream
- [ ] Gantt built with realistic durations, dependencies, and the critical path identified
- [ ] Committee and approval cadence laid in: seller-side, buyer-side, regulatory, third-party consents
- [ ] Risk register operational with severity, owner, mitigation, status, target close date
- [ ] Weekly cadence (deal-team Monday, workstream mid-week, client end-of-week, written memo) established
- [ ] Critical path called out each week — not buried in the Gantt
- [ ] Sign-to-close period has its own plan with closing-conditions tracker and Day-1 communications
- [ ] Post-mortem scheduled at close, not optional

## Common Failure Modes

- **Gantt as decoration.** Building a beautiful timeline at kickoff and never updating it. By week 4 it is fiction.
- **No named critical path.** Treating every workstream as equally important. The team disperses attention; the binding constraint slips.
- **Risk register that grows but never shrinks.** Issues are added; nothing ever gets closed with a resolution note. Becomes noise, gets ignored.
- **Committee cadence by exception.** "We'll get IC approval when we need it." IC reschedules into a deal-killing two-week gap.
- **Regulatory wishful thinking.** Assuming standard clearance when the deal mechanics suggest Phase II or Second Request. Adds 60–90 days to timeline that the original Gantt didn't reflect.
- **Sign-to-close fade.** Deal team disbands at signing. Closing conditions miss target dates. Working capital adjustment becomes a brawl because nobody owned the tracker.
- **No written weekly memo.** Verbal updates only. By week 6 nobody can reconstruct what was decided, when, and by whom.
- **No post-mortem.** Lessons stay in the deal team's heads, leave with the team, and have to be relearned on the next mandate.

## Deliverable

A process plan covering process-type recommendation with trade-offs, a workstream architecture with named owners, a Gantt with the critical path explicitly identified, a committee and approval cadence, a risk register that is live and updated weekly, a weekly client memo template and discipline, a sign-to-close plan with closing-conditions tracker, and a post-mortem framework scheduled at close.
