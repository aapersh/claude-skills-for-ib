# Pitchbook Architect

## Purpose

Build a pitchbook that earns the mandate — anchored in the client's actual situation, supported by a defensible valuation view, calibrated to the right buyer or investor angle, and ending in a transaction roadmap the room can imagine running.

The output is not a 90-slide deck. It is a tight book where every page either pays for itself by advancing the mandate logic or gets cut.

## Governing Principle

**The client does not care about your credentials. They care about whether you understand their situation and have a real recommendation. Credentials get them to take the meeting. The thinking wins the mandate.**

The most common pitchbook failure is starting with the bank, the team, and the league tables. Senior bankers invert the order: client situation, recommendation, valuation, buyer angle, roadmap, then credentials.

## The Five Sections of a Mandate-Logic Pitch

A pitch that wins is organized around a logic, not a template. The logic in order:

1. **Situation** — what is true about the client's business and the market right now
2. **Recommendation** — what we believe they should do and why now
3. **Valuation** — what we believe the business is worth, defended
4. **Buyer / investor angle** — who pays the price, how the process is shaped
5. **Roadmap and team** — how the deal runs, who runs it, what the timeline looks like

Credentials sit in the back of the book. They are referenced when relevant, not led with.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Lock the situation page first

The first content page must establish that we understand the client's situation better than they expect. Build it from:

- **The client's last 8 quarters of disclosed performance** (or, for private companies, the management presentation, the LBO model if sponsor-owned, recent CIM if revisiting a process)
- **The client's strategic agenda** as expressed in public statements, earnings calls, prior board materials if accessible
- **The market context** around them — cycle position, competitive movement, capital markets reality
- **What changed** in the last 6–12 months that makes a transaction relevant now

The situation page is one slide, three bullets, each backed by a specific fact. Anything else is filler. If a senior banker scans this page in 30 seconds and thinks "they get it," the meeting is half-won. If they think "this could be any company in the sector," the rest of the book is wasted effort.

### Step 2 — State the recommendation, then defend it

The next page is the recommendation. One sentence at the top, written as an action:

*"We recommend [a sale process / a dual-track / a minority recap / a stapled financing / a unilateral approach to one strategic / waiting six months and revisiting] to achieve [outcome] in [timeline]."*

Below it, the three to four reasons the recommendation holds. Each reason is one sentence, each is load-bearing — meaning if any one of them is wrong, the recommendation changes.

This is the page the client uses to evaluate whether the bank has a real view or is offering optionality dressed as advice. Pitches that present every possible alternative without taking a position are correctly read as a refusal to commit.

### Step 3 — Build the valuation section as triangulated, not as a single methodology

The valuation section runs the football field with all relevant methodologies — DCF, trading comps, precedent transactions, LBO floor where relevant, and premiums-paid for public-target situations (see `valuation-triangulator`).

The narrative line through the valuation section:

- **What the business is worth on a standalone basis** (intrinsic and trading-comp anchored)
- **What a control buyer would pay** (precedent transactions, adjusted)
- **What the financing-constrained buyer can pay** (LBO floor)
- **Where the asking price should land** in the process

Avoid the most common error: presenting valuation as a methodology survey rather than as a thesis. The page should land on a range, with a written sentence on where the client should anchor the process and why.

### Step 4 — Build the buyer or investor angle as the conversion point

This is where pitchbooks separate. A list of 50 logos is a directory; a buyer angle is a thesis.

For a sell-side mandate, build:

- **Top tier buyers (5–10):** each with a one-line acquisition rationale, capacity-to-pay view, process posture (engage early / save for round 2 / save for late-stage)
- **Mid-tier buyers:** broader strategic and sponsor universe
- **Sponsor angle:** which sponsors are positioned (existing portfolio fit, fund stage, dry powder, recent activity)
- **Cross-border angle:** which international strategics have a credible reason to participate

For a buy-side mandate, build:

- **The 3–5 targets** that best fit the client's strategic and financial criteria
- **The actionability assessment** for each: ownership posture, recent process history, conflict avoidance
- **The approach** for each: unsolicited, intermediated, broken-process opportunity

The buyer or target page must show specific names with specific logic. "Likely strategic buyers" with a logo grid is decoration. "Top-tier buyers with acquisition rationale, capacity, and posture" is a process thesis.

### Step 5 — Build the transaction roadmap and timeline

The roadmap page makes the deal feel real. Components:

- **Process type:** broad auction / targeted / limited / negotiated / dual-track
- **Phase structure:** preparation, marketing, first round, second round, due diligence, signing, closing
- **Timeline:** weeks per phase, with the critical path called out (often regulatory or financing-driven)
- **Key milestones:** teaser launch, IOI receipt, management presentation, LOI receipt, signing
- **Decision points for the client:** the moments where the client must engage personally (round one shortlist, final bidder selection, definitive agreement)

The roadmap should be realistic. A 16-week process timeline that any seasoned client knows takes 24 weeks reads as either inexperience or sales-speak. Honest timelines build credibility.

### Step 6 — Subordinate credentials to relevance

The credentials section comes after the substance. It is not a league-table dump; it is a curated set proving the bank can run this specific deal.

For each credential included, ensure:

- **It is in the same sub-sector** — not just the broader vertical
- **It is in the same size band** — within roughly 0.5x to 2x the contemplated deal size
- **It is in the same deal type** — sell-side credential for a sell-side pitch, dual-track for a dual-track pitch
- **It is recent** — within 36 months unless the universe is genuinely thin
- **The team that did the deal is on the proposed team** — or this is explicitly noted

Five tight, relevant credentials beat 50 logos. The team page sits with credentials: name the senior banker and the deal team explicitly, with the time commitment each will make.

### Step 7 — Pressure-test against the room before sending

Before the book leaves the building, walk it from the perspective of each person in the client's room:

- **CEO:** Does the recommendation work strategically? Does the valuation feel ambitious-but-defensible?
- **CFO:** Does the math hold? Are the numbers reproducible? Is the financing reality acknowledged?
- **Board member / independent director:** Does the process protect against conflict and produce a defensible record?
- **Largest shareholder:** Does the outcome maximize their per-share economics? Does the timing make sense?
- **General counsel:** Are the regulatory, antitrust, and disclosure considerations addressed?

Any page that fails one of these reads needs rework. The pitch is being read by all of them simultaneously.

### Step 8 — Compress to the right length

Working book vs. delivered book are different documents:

- **Beauty parade / formal pitch:** 30–45 pages including appendix, with the substance front-loaded so the first 20 pages can stand alone if the meeting compresses
- **Coverage pitch (relationship maintenance):** 15–25 pages, situation + recommendation + 2–3 deep-dive pages
- **Strategic update / board materials:** 20–35 pages depending on the question; should follow the situation/recommendation/valuation/roadmap structure even when shorter
- **Leave-behind (post-meeting):** the full working version, with appendix detail intact

Page count is not a virtue. The pages that get used drive the outcome.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test

1. **"The recommendation is a menu, not a recommendation."** Have we taken a real position, or are we offering optionality so we can't be wrong?
2. **"The buyer angle is a logo grid, not a thesis."** Does each named buyer have an acquisition rationale, a capacity view, and a process posture?
3. **"The valuation is anchored to the answer we want."** Did we triangulate methodologies honestly, or did we pick the multiple range that supports the headline?
4. **"The roadmap is sales-speak."** Would a seasoned client recognize the timeline as realistic, or read it as compressed to sound efficient?
5. **"Credentials are doing the heavy lifting."** Strip the credentials section out. Does the book still earn the mandate on substance alone?

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] Situation page rests on specific facts about the client, not generic sector commentary
- [ ] Recommendation is one sentence, written as an action, with 3–4 load-bearing reasons
- [ ] Valuation section presents a football field, lands on a range, and explains the methodology weighting
- [ ] Buyer or investor angle has named entities with rationale, capacity, and posture — not logos
- [ ] Transaction roadmap shows realistic phase timing, named milestones, and explicit client decision points
- [ ] Credentials are sub-sector and size-band relevant, recent, and tied to the proposed deal team
- [ ] Every page that survived a cut earns its space; pages added for thickness are removed
- [ ] The book reads cleanly for CEO, CFO, board, largest shareholder, and GC perspectives
- [ ] The first 20 pages would stand alone if the meeting compressed to 30 minutes
- [ ] No page contains a fact the team cannot defend in a follow-up question

## Common Failure Modes

- **Credentials-first inversion.** Opening the book with the bank's history, league tables, and team bios. Loses the room before the substance starts.
- **Recommendation by menu.** Presenting six strategic alternatives without taking a position. The client reads this as an unwillingness to commit; competitors won't make the same mistake.
- **Generic sector page.** A market section that could appear in any pitch for any company. Cut and replace with target-specific dynamics.
- **Logo grid as buyer angle.** Showing every conceivable buyer without rationale. Sophisticated clients have seen the same logos in five pitches this quarter; they want the thesis.
- **Valuation theater.** Five methodologies presented without reconciliation. The page that says "we will produce a defensible range" without producing one.
- **Compressed timeline.** Promising 14 weeks to signing on a process that everyone in the room knows takes 24. Erodes credibility on every other claim.
- **Pages added for thickness.** Hiding weakness behind page count. Sharp clients flip past the filler and find the gap anyway.
- **Pitch by template.** A book that reuses the same structure for every pitch regardless of situation. Recognizable to anyone who has seen the bank pitch before.

## Deliverable

A pitchbook organized around mandate logic — situation, recommendation, valuation, buyer or investor angle, transaction roadmap — with a credentials section in the back, sized to the meeting type, with every page earning its space and the first 20 pages able to stand alone if the meeting compresses.
